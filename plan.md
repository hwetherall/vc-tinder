1. The data model is the keystone — design it to mirror Attio, not your CSV. Your flat CSV row mashes together three different Attio objects: the fund (a Company), the partner (a Person), and your assessment of them (custom attributes + a pipeline entry). If your backend tables mirror Attio's shape from day one, the export becomes a near-mechanical upsert instead of a translation layer you have to maintain. Concretely, in Insforge Postgres:
firm          id, name, website, hq_location, aum, stage_focus,
              thesis_tags[], lead_capable, is_fund (gate flag),
              status: new|researching|researched|needs_review|scored|approved|synced,
              attio_company_id (nullable until synced)

contact       id, firm_id, name, title, linkedin, email,
              is_decision_maker, intro_path, intro_owner,
              attio_person_id

score         id, firm_id, thesis_fit/25, network/25, lead_capability/25,
              location/15, gravitas/10, composite (generated col), tier (1-5),
              scored_by: ai|human, model_version, scored_at

flag          id, firm_id, field, claim, confidence: low|med|high,
              evidence_url, status: open|confirmed|rejected, resolved_by

evidence      id, firm_id, source_url, fetched_at, raw_markdown,
              snippet, used_for (which field this supports)
The flag and evidence tables are what make this trustworthy rather than a black box. Every score cites the evidence row it came from; every uncertain inference becomes a flag the human resolves in your UI. That's your step 3, and it's the part that earns the human's trust in the other four. Use Insforge's pgvector here too: embed name + website + thesis so when you add new VCs later, you can catch "is this firm already in the system under a slightly different name?" before re-researching it.
2. Firecrawl and Exa are not either/or — they own different stages. Firecrawl is extraction-first: search-and-scrape in one call, returning clean markdown or structured JSON, with an /extract endpoint that takes a natural-language schema. Exa is discovery-first: neural search, plus a "find similar" feature that retrieves pages like a given URL, and Websets for sourcing people and companies. So: for a VC you already have, point Firecrawl at their site + a couple of searches and pull a structured profile (check size, stage, recent leads, partner focus). For finding new VCs — your scaling goal — seed Exa's find-similar with your Tier-1 firms and let it surface lookalikes, which then dedup against pgvector and enter the same pipeline. Firecrawl is flat-priced (1 credit = 1 page, ~$83/100k), while Exa's per-search cost varies with complexity, so the cost-predictable workhorse (Firecrawl) does the bulk extraction and Exa does the cheaper, occasional discovery sweeps. 3. Run the AI server-side through Insforge, not in the browser. This is the reason Insforge is a genuinely good pick here rather than just a database. It bundles a Postgres database, storage, edge functions, a vector database (pgvector), and an AI model gateway in one platform, all operable by an agent over MCP. So an edge function does: pull firm rows where status='researched' → send the evidence markdown to the model gateway with your rubric → write back five sub-scores, a tier, and any flags. Keys stay server-side, the work is resumable, and your front-end just reads tables. One honest caveat: Insforge is young — YC spring 2026 batch, public since November 2025, so you're trading Supabase's maturity for a more agent-native fit. For an internal tool that's a fine trade; just know you may hit rough edges a 5-year-old platform wouldn't have. byteiotaPasquale Pillitteri
4. For the Attio sync, use the REST API for the batch path — not MCP. This distinction trips people up. Attio's official hosted MCP server connects over OAuth and is built for conversational, in-assistant CRM actions like "find these companies and add them to a list." That's great for ad-hoc moves, but a nightly upsert of 100 scored firms is a programmatic batch, and you want deterministic, idempotent writes from your Insforge edge function straight to the REST API. Attio rate-limits around 100 reads/sec and 25 writes/sec with extra score-based limits on list queries, so batch with backoff and store the returned attio_company_id/attio_person_id on your rows to make re-syncs upserts instead of duplicates. Map firm→Company, contact→Person, scores+tier→custom attributes, and your pipeline into a "Series A Targets" list. Keep the MCP connection around for the "Claude, bump these three to Tier 1 in Attio" moments. Attio DocsTruto
On build order, resist doing all five stages at once. The thing that de-risks everything is one VC flowing end-to-end through a thin vertical slice: pick a firm you already know cold (say, Accel), run it through Firecrawl → a hardcoded edge function that scores with your rubric → write to the tables → render in your existing swipe card → push one record to a test Attio workspace. Once a single firm makes the whole journey, widening to 100 and adding discovery is mostly volume, not new architecture.
